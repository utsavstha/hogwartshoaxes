Gringotts Gorbles: A Goblin's Lament on Wage Inequality and the SPEW Revival

April 3, 2025 | By Bathilda Bagshot (Granddaughter of *the* Bathilda Bagshot)

By Bathilda Bagshot (Granddaughter of the Bathilda Bagshot)

Is the wizarding world truly equal? While we all enjoy the benefits of magic – instant cleaning, self-stirring cauldrons, and the occasional levitation of Dudley Dursley’s pudding – a chasm of inequality yawns beneath our polished wands. We’re not talking about pure-blood snobbery (though Merlin knows that’s still a problem). We’re talking about cold, hard Galleons.

Take, for instance, our esteemed goblin colleagues at Gringotts. While the Ministry enjoys bottomless coffers (likely refilled with questionable accounting spells), rumours swirl of goblins struggling to afford sufficient polishing paste for their impressively sharp teeth. Grunt Gringotts employee, Grilk (who wished to remain anonymous for fear of being turned into a particularly nasty paperweight), whispered, “We guard the gold, but we see very little of it ourselves! The dragons get better dental!”

And then there’s the house-elf situation. While some, like Dobby (may his sock-filled afterlife be peaceful), found liberation, countless others remain indentured servants, toiling away for families who still believe that snapping their fingers constitutes a reasonable payment. A recent survey by the Wizarding Statistics Bureau revealed that the average house-elf earns approximately zero Galleons per year, plus room and board (which, admittedly, is often under the stairs). This disparity has spurred a revival of sorts for S.P.E.W., the Society for the Promotion of Elfish Welfare. Hermione Granger-Weasley, the driving force behind the movement (and Minister for Magic’s right-hand witch), declined to comment directly, citing ongoing negotiations with the Wizengamot regarding the ‘Elfish Emancipation Act.’ However, sources say she’s been seen knitting increasingly aggressive-looking elf-sized jumpers.

Even within the wizarding community, inequality persists. Quidditch players, particularly those who endorse broom companies, earn fortunes rivaling those of the Malfoys (though thankfully with less… ethical baggage). Meanwhile, Herbologists and Charms specialists often struggle to secure funding for groundbreaking research. Professor Sprout, when approached for comment, simply brandished a mandrake at this reporter, though a colleague suggested she muttered something about ‘potions ingredients costing a fortune these days.’

So, what can be done? The Wizengamot is debating several proposals, including a progressive taxation system (much to the chagrin of those with overflowing vaults) and increased funding for magical education, ensuring that even those from modest backgrounds have the opportunity to pursue their dreams – and perhaps, one day, afford that self-stirring cauldron. Whether these measures will be enough to bridge the gap remains to be seen. But one thing is clear: the wizarding world, like its Muggle counterpart, must grapple with the uncomfortable truth that not everyone is enjoying the same slice of the Treacle Tart.
