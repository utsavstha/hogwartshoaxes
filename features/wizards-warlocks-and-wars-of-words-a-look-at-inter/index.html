Wizards, Warlocks, and Wars of Words: A Look at International Wizarding Relations (and Why Norbert the Norwegian Ridgeback is NOT a Diplomat)

April 30, 2025 | By Bathilda Bagshot

By Bathilda Bagshot (Probably Retired, But Still Nosy)

The International Confederation of Wizards (ICW), that august and occasionally baffling body, has seen its fair share of squabbles. While Muggles grapple with trade tariffs and…whatever a ‘TikTok’ is, we wizards face significantly more magical (and occasionally explosive) diplomatic challenges.

Recent tensions between Romania and Great Britain over dragon rights have been brewing hotter than a cauldron of Firewhisky. Sources claim that Charlie Weasley, the beloved dragon enthusiast, nearly ignited a diplomatic incident at a recent ICW summit after accusing Romanian delegates of ‘under-buttering their Flobberworms.’ While Mr. Weasley maintains this was a matter of crucial dragon nutrition, many see it as a thinly veiled power play. Let’s not forget the infamous ‘Norbert Incident’ of ‘91 when Hagrid, in his infinite wisdom, attempted to negotiate a treaty using a Norwegian Ridgeback as a ’living olive branch’. The resulting dragon fire scorched several ministry official’s robes and permanently altered Minister Fudge’s hairstyle. The ICW passed a resolution banning the use of potentially flammable fauna in diplomatic negotiations shortly thereafter.

Further West, the French Ministry of Magic is engaged in a passive-aggressive charm-off with the Bulgarians over whose Veela choir is truly superior. Sources indicate that Fleur Delacour (now Mrs. Weasley, and apparently a master of the withering glance) has been drafted in as a consultant for the British contingent, lending her expertise to ensure the UK remains neutral and, more importantly, immune to any excessive levels of alluring song. I wouldn’t want another Yule Ball incident.

Across the pond, MACUSA (Magical Congress of the United States of America) finds itself embroiled in a bizarre quibble with the Canadian Ministry. Apparently, an overly enthusiastic Thunderbird migration caused significant damage to several maple syrup farms in Quebec. The resulting sticky situation (literally) has led to demands for Thunderbird flight restrictions and a formal apology written in calligraphy – preferably on parchment made from Canadian birch bark.

But perhaps the most pressing issue facing the ICW is the ongoing debate regarding the ethical use of Gnomes in garden security. A growing movement, spearheaded by Hermione Granger (formerly Granger-Weasley, champion of house-elves and all things unjustly treated), argues that the current gnome-tossing practices are barbaric and warrant stricter regulations. Traditionalist factions, particularly within the German Ministry of Magic, are vehemently opposed, citing centuries of ‘gnome-handling heritage’. The issue is currently stuck in committee, primarily because nobody wants to be stuck in a room with a large number of angry gnomes. One can’t say I blame them. After all, a well-aimed gnome can ruin a perfectly good hat.

So, as we navigate these murky waters of wizarding geopolitics, let us remember the importance of diplomacy, tolerance, and perhaps, a generous application of Undetectable Extension Charms to accommodate the inevitable avalanche of paperwork. And for Merlin’s sake, keep your dragons (and gnomes) on a leash.
