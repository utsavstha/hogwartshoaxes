Fibres of Deceit: Can We Trust What We Read (Especially When Goblins Write It)?

April 2, 2025 | By Beatrice Butterburrow

By Beatrice Butterburrow, Special Correspondent

The age of the Wizarding Wireless Network (WWN) has brought unparalleled access to information. Need the latest Quaffle scores? Boom, there they are. Want to know if Gilderoy Lockhart is actually opening a new charm school? (Please Merlin, no.) The WWN delivers. But with this deluge of data comes a darker current: the rise of misinformation. Sound familiar? Think of it as a particularly nasty Bubotuber pus – prolific, unpleasant, and shockingly hard to get rid of.

We’ve seen it firsthand. Remember Rita Skeeter’s early reports on Harry Potter? Entirely fabricated, fuelled by Quick-Quotes Quill and a healthy dose of malice. Now imagine that multiplied a thousandfold, fuelled by… well, the Goblin Liberation Front.

“The biggest problem is the speed at which falsehoods spread,” explains Hermione Granger, Head of the Department of Magical Law Enforcement’s Anti-Misinformation Taskforce. “A tweet – I mean, a ‘witch’ – can travel around the world faster than a Firebolt. By the time we debunk a claim that, say, Gringotts is secretly hoarding all the chocolate frogs, half the wizarding world is already emptying their accounts and rioting outside Honeydukes.” (Granger sighed, looking remarkably tired for someone who only turned 44 this year).

The parallels to Goblin propaganda are undeniable. The historical narrative surrounding the Goblin Rebellions has been systematically twisted by certain…elements. The notion that Goblins are inherently greedy and treacherous? Largely perpetuated by wizards. But are Goblins ALWAYS angels singing carols? Well, no. Let’s not forget Ragnuk the First, who wasn’t exactly known for his philanthropy.

“It’s all about understanding the source,” says Professor Binns, who, despite teaching history with the enthusiasm of a damp sock, occasionally stumbles upon a relevant point. “Who is spreading this information? What are their motivations? Is it a reputable source like ‘Witch Weekly’, or is it a dodgy blog called ‘GoblinGossip.com’, run by someone suspiciously named ‘Grindtooth McGreedy’?” (Binns then drifted off into a lecture about the Goblin Wars of 1747, proving his ability to bore even himself).

So, what can we do? Firstly, be critical! Don’t believe everything you read, even if it comes from a trusted friend (especially if that friend is Ron Weasley and the information involves free food). Secondly, check your sources! Look for corroborating evidence, and be wary of claims that are too good to be true (like Lockhart’s supposed dragon-slaying abilities). Finally, report misinformation! The Ministry of Magic has dedicated an entire department to combating this menace. (Though rumour has it, half the employees are just playing Exploding Snap).

In the age of the WWN, the responsibility of truth lies with each of us. Don’t let falsehoods flourish. Be vigilant, be informed, and for Merlin’s sake, don’t believe everything a Goblin tells you, especially about interest rates.
